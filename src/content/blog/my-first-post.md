Back in July 2021, I started my first full-time job, and time has indeed flown by quickly. It's now July 2026. If you had told me five years ago that I would be working in a sales organisation, I would have laughed it off. It was not in the plans. Life is unpredictable, but the journey of how I got to where I am today is something I would like to share in this post. For those who aren't too interested in what I have been doing for the past five years, feel free to skip ahead to the other technical blogs! I hope you find them useful and interesting.

## From Database Engineering to Sales Engineering

Right out of college, I started off as a database engineer at Grab. I was fascinated by things like B+ trees, write-ahead logs, and database locking. There is a lot of technical depth to understand in databases.

Then I began to wonder if what I was doing was too niche. I wanted to explore other parts of the technical world instead of focusing solely on databases, so I transitioned into a site-reliability engineer, managing the company's Elasticsearch clusters. That gave me the breadth I was looking for, as SREs are expected to be jacks of all trades. The DevOps and cloud-native skillsets I picked up were valuable and versatile.

Around 2023, platform engineering was the hype, said to be the next evolution of DevOps. I had the opportunity to join TikTok as a platform engineer, building out the company's data warehouse platform. There, I got to deepen my expertise in Kubernetes and data warehousing.

By then, I had spent about 3.5 years in development and operational roles. They were very technical, and I enjoyed them. But I was curious to see whether I would like a more customer-facing role, as I thought it would present a different kind of challenge. So when the right opportunity came, I decided to join IBM's client engineering team. We are part of the sales org, where we mainly focus on executing proof-of-concepts and serving as the technical experts.

After 1.5 years in this role, I can say that being part of sales means things are very dynamic in nature. No two days are the same, and that keeps things exciting. Initially, I was worried that I would lose my technical edge when I transitioned to sales, but I am lucky that the role still exposes me to many technical challenges.

## Reflections

Each of my roles taught me many things, and I got to work with great colleagues at all three companies. That is something to be grateful for.

Grab, as my first company, exposed me to production and live environments, with real transactions and scale. TikTok taught me that executing with speed matters in a high-growth company. IBM sales taught me that companies buy technology for its business value, not its functions and features.

## What I'll try to write in my blog

In tech, things advance so quickly that we constantly need to keep up with industry trends. That was the primary motivator for me to finally start this blog: to document my experiences, musings, observations, and challenges. I hope it will be useful and that I am able to contribute back to the tech community.
